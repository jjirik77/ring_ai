[ PROJECT TURING-2025 ]

In 1950, mathematician Alan Turing published a paper titled "Computing Machinery and Intelligence." It opened with a deceptively simple question: "Can machines think?"

Turing proposed a test — now called the Turing Test — to answer it. An examiner holds text conversations with two parties: one human, one machine. If the examiner cannot reliably tell which is which, the machine passes.

In this activity, you will read 8 conversation exchanges. For each one, you must decide: is the respondent a human or an AI? And more importantly — what specific behavior tipped you off?

Turing Test

A test proposed by Alan Turing in 1950 in which a human examiner determines whether a conversational partner is a human or a machine, based solely on their text responses.

ELIZA Effect

The human tendency to unconsciously attribute human-like emotions, intentions, and understanding to AI systems — even when no real intelligence is present.

Moravec's Paradox

The observation that tasks humans find easy (like recognizing a face) are extremely difficult for AI, while tasks humans find difficult (like complex math) are relatively easy for AI.

THE ELIZA EFFECT — A PAUSE

In 1966, MIT researcher Joseph Weizenbaum created ELIZA — a simple chatbot that mimicked a therapist. ELIZA had no real understanding of language.

Weizenbaum was disturbed to find that people began forming emotional attachments to it. They believed it understood them.
