Pre-Lab Briefing

What is a Decision Threshold?

AI models rarely think in pure black and white. Instead of a simple "yes" or "no", they calculate a Probability Score or confidence rating (e.g., "This email has an 85% chance of being spam").

To turn this numerical score into a real-world action, humans must define a Decision Threshold—the specific line where we choose to act.

Meet the Confusion Matrix

Every threshold decision we make leads to direct, real-world consequences. No AI or automated system is perfectly accurate in every edge case.

Depending on whether our school smoke alarm rings or stays silent, there are exactly four possible outcomes. These outcomes form a grid called a Confusion Matrix.

TP

True Positive

The cafeteria has a real fire, and the alarm rings. Students evacuate safely. 🎉

FP

False Positive

It's just burnt toast, but the alarm rings anyway. Students shiver outside. 🙄

FN

False Negative

The school is on fire, but the alarm stays silent. Extremely dangerous! 😱

TN

True Negative

The air is perfectly safe, and the alarm is quiet. Normal school day. 📚

Precision vs. Recall Tug-of-War

Because real-world data overlaps, optimizing a decision model is a perpetual balancing act.

Prioritizing Precision means you want your positive alerts to be extremely reliable. Prioritizing Recall means you want to catch as many positive cases as possible.
