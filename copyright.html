Who Owns This?

"Copyright law meets the digital world."

Everything you see online was created by someone. Music, photos, videos, writing, code — all of it is protected by copyright law the moment it is created. Copyright gives creators the exclusive right to copy, distribute, and profit from their work.

But digital technology made copying nearly effortless and free. Downloading a song, screenshotting an image, sampling a beat, using an AI tool trained on copyrighted art — these acts happen billions of times a day. Some are legal. Some are not. Many exist in a grey zone.

"Fair use" is a legal exception that allows limited use of copyrighted material without permission — for education, commentary, parody, news reporting. But the line between fair use and infringement is not always clear.

"Piracy" is the unauthorized reproduction or distribution of copyrighted material, typically for free or for profit, without the creator's permission.

Ruling Types
Legal Principles

✅ FAIR USE: The use is legal without permission. It serves a transformative, educational, critical, or commentary purpose, and does not harm the original creator's market.

🔵 LICENSED: The creator has explicitly granted permission through a license (e.g., Creative Commons, royalty-free, open source). Use is permitted within the license terms.

🚫 PIRACY: The use is unauthorized and violates copyright. The creator's rights are being infringed — whether or not money changes hands.

⚠️ UNCLEAR: The case falls in a legal grey zone. Courts have disagreed, or the technology is new enough that clear precedent doesn't yet exist.

⚙️ Transformative Use: Does the new work add something new — commentary, parody, critique, new meaning? Or does it simply reproduce the original?

📏 Proportion: How much of the original work was used? Using one sentence from a book differs from reproducing an entire chapter.

💰 Market Harm: Does the use harm the original creator's ability to sell their work? A free download that replaces a purchase is different from a scholarly quote.

🎓 Educational Purpose: Use for nonprofit education, research, criticism, or news reporting receives more protection than commercial use.

Special Case: AI Art

Active litigation and the copyright frontier.

Context

In 2023 and 2024, multiple lawsuits were filed against AI companies by artists, authors, and media companies. The central claim: AI image generators (Midjourney, Stable Diffusion) and AI writing tools (ChatGPT) were trained on billions of copyrighted images and texts — without the creators' permission and without payment. The AI then generates new content in the style of those creators.

You read about the ChatGPT/Studio Ghibli case in your Unit 1.2 warm-up. This is that case — now you'll analyze it in three parts.

Scenario: An AI company scraped millions of copyrighted images from the internet to train their image generation model. Artists did not consent. No payment was made. The AI can now generate images in the style of any artist whose work was included.

⚠️ UNCLEAR / LITIGATION

Actual Status: UNCLEAR. This is the central unresolved copyright question of the AI era. Courts in the U.S. and EU are actively deciding it. Some judges have dismissed claims; others have allowed them to proceed. The core question: is scraping copyrighted content for AI training "transformative" (suggesting Fair Use) or does it harm artists' markets?

Scenario: A user asks the same AI to generate an image "in the style of Studio Ghibli." The AI produces an image that has never existed before but is clearly recognizable as matching Studio Ghibli's visual style. The user shares it on social media.

⚠️ UNCLEAR / LITIGATION

Actual Status: UNCLEAR. Copyright does not protect artistic "style" — only specific works. An image that resembles Ghibli's style but does not reproduce a specific Ghibli frame is unlikely to infringe copyright under current law. However, this question is being actively litigated, and some legal scholars argue that style mimicry at AI scale crosses a line.
